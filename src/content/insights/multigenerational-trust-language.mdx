The first time a trust's distributive language is read with money on the line, it is almost never read by the lawyer who drafted it. The drafting attorney has retired, or moved firms, or, in the longest-lived structures, passed away. The trustee, the family, and the next generation of advisors are left to interpret intent from sentences that were written for a different tax regime and a different family circumstance.

This is the part of estate planning that the binder cannot solve. A document that is correct in 2008 may be merely lawful in 2026, and the difference between *correct* and *lawful* is where families lose money, lose time, and lose the relationships that hold the structure together.

Heritage Strategies reads trust language sentence by sentence and color-codes it against the family's actual balance sheet, the prevailing tax regime, and the disposition the family actually wants. We refresh the read every year. Specifically:

1. We map each schedule to the trusts it funds.
2. We flag language that no longer expresses current intent in red.
3. We flag language that has aged well in black.
4. We surface the questions the trustee will need to answer, before the trustee is the one being asked.

It is a small, persistent discipline. It is not a substitute for counsel. But it gives counsel something concrete to argue against when the moment comes.
